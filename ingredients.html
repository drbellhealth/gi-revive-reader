GI Revive Powder Ingredients

A line-by-line look at what's inside Designs for Health GI Revive Powder, including active components and excipients.

The full ingredient list is on the tub and the brand's site. The short version is below — and it is a deliberate mix, with each piece working on a different layer of your gut.

Active Ingredients

What is actually in it:

  • L-glutamine (about 3 g a scoop) — food for the cells lining your small intestine; the main repair ingredient
  • N-acetyl glucosamine (about 1 g) — supports the mucus layer over the cells (worth knowing: some comes from shellfish)
  • Citrus pectin (about 700 mg) — a gentle soluble fiber that also helps the mucus layer
  • DGL licorice (about 500 mg) — calms inflammation in the stomach and upper gut, without regular licorice's blood-pressure effect
  • Aloe, slippery elm, marshmallow root (about 250 / 200 / 200 mg) — soothing herbs that coat an irritated surface
  • Zinc carnosine (about 75 mg) — a gut-specific zinc and the best-studied piece for healing the lining
  • Chamomile, cat's claw, MSM, quercetin, prune powder (smaller amounts) — extra calming and gentle-motility support

Other Ingredients (Excipients)

Because it is a flavored powder and not a capsule, there is not really a filler list to scrutinize — it is more about taste and how it mixes. It has a mild, slightly sweet flavor, dissolves nicely in water if you give it about half a minute, and is not gritty. Some people prefer it in a little unsweetened almond milk or apple juice. If you are sensitive to a particular sweetener or flavor, your current label is the place to look, since they adjust it over time.

Allergens and Sensitivities

The allergy notes here come from the herbs and the glucosamine, not the usual capsule stuff. Chamomile is in the ragweed/daisy family, so a serious ragweed allergy can cross over. Some of the glucosamine is shellfish-based, which matters if you have a serious shellfish allergy — check the label or pick a different formula. Histamine-sensitive people sometimes react to the chamomile or licorice. Your current label carries the official allergen statement, so let that be the final word.

How Ingredients Compare to Similar Products

Two things are worth comparing. Powder versus capsules: both exist, the powder is a bigger dose and coats the upper gut more directly, the capsules are easier to travel with — so it is dose and contact versus convenience. And the full blend versus something simpler: if you have a lot of allergies or a really sensitive gut, you might do better on just glutamine, DGL, and zinc carnosine (or those individually) than the long herb list. Also good to know what it is not — it is not an antimicrobial like GI Microb-X and not a digestive enzyme like Digestzymes; it is a repair-and-soothe formula for the lining. The right pick depends on your goal and how reactive your gut is.
