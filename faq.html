GI Revive Powder FAQ

Quick answers to the questions visitors most often ask about Designs for Health GI Revive Powder.

What is GI Revive Powder, and what does it do?

It is a powder you mix in water that helps your gut lining rebuild. The glutamine feeds the cells lining your intestine, the aloe/slippery elm/marshmallow soothe the surface, the DGL calms upper-gut inflammation, and the zinc carnosine supports the actual repair. It works on the barrier rather than just covering up a symptom.

What is in it, in plain terms?

Mostly L-glutamine (about 3 g), N-acetyl glucosamine (about 1 g), citrus pectin (about 700 mg), DGL (about 500 mg), aloe/slippery elm/marshmallow, and zinc carnosine (about 75 mg), plus smaller amounts of chamomile, cat's claw, MSM, quercetin, and prune powder. Check your tub for the exact numbers.

Is this the right fit for me?

It fits best if your gut got beaten up by NSAIDs, never recovered after food poisoning or antibiotics, or you have IBS-type bloating with foods that suddenly bother you. It is often paired with a probiotic after antibiotics.

Powder or capsules?

Both work. The powder is a bigger dose and coats the upper gut better; the capsules are easier to carry. Go powder for the strongest effect, capsules if convenience wins.

How do I take it, and why before food?

Usually one scoop in about 8 oz of water once a day on an empty stomach — bedtime is ideal because your gut repairs overnight and you want the ingredients on the lining without food in the way. Two hours after dinner or first thing in the morning also work. For rough patches, some people take it twice a day for the first 4 to 6 weeks.

How long until I feel a difference?

Often a little more stomach comfort in the first week, less bloating and more food tolerance by weeks two to four, and fuller results by weeks four to eight. A complete repair usually takes three to four months — faster if you fix the cause too.

Is there anyone who should not take it?

Check with a clinician first if you have a serious ragweed/daisy allergy (chamomile), a serious shellfish allergy (some glucosamine is shellfish-based), take immunosuppressants (cat's claw), have an active ulcer, have advanced kidney or liver disease (the glutamine), or are pregnant or nursing.

Should I take a probiotic with it?

Often yes — something like ProbioMed 100 is a common pairing, especially after antibiotics. Just space the probiotic a few hours from GI Revive so they do not bind.
